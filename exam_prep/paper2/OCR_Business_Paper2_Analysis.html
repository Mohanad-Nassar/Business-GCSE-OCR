Question Type & Command Word Analysis

Key pattern: Q16–Q17 each use a layered structure of State/Identify (1–2 marks) → Explain/Calculate (2–3 marks) → Analyse (3–6 marks) → Recommend/Evaluate (3–7 marks). Q18 always culminates in the 9-mark starred Evaluate question. Section A MCQs (1 mark each, 15 questions) test definitional and calculation knowledge across all topics.

TOPIC CODE 5.3.1–5.3.6

Dominated by Calculate commands — gross profit margin, net profit margin, average profit, percentage change in revenue. Frequently appears as a 2–3 mark calculation sub-question. Also appears as Analyse (3 marks) when asking students to use financial data. The 7-mark starred format has been used to evaluate the usefulness of financial data (2023 Q18). Mark allocations: 1, 2, 3, 6, 7, 9.

Part 04

Common Errors — What Students Get Wrong

All errors identified directly from examiner mark scheme guidance notes (TV = Too Vague, No Reward statements, NB warnings, and guidance restrictions).

Error Type — Cash vs Profit Confusion

Questions on cash flow, importance of cash, or cash management
Why students get it wrong: Students confuse cash with profit, writing "a business needs cash to make a profit" or discussing profit when asked about cash. The examiner explicitly states "No reward for answers which confuse cash and profit." Students also describe long-term investment uses of cash rather than short-term operational needs.
Correct approach: Cash is needed to pay short-term debts and expenses (wages, rent, suppliers). Without it, a business cannot continue operating even if it is profitable. Never write "to make profit" as a reason for needing cash. Focus on liquidity and survival — e.g., "Cash allows the business to pay its suppliers on time, preventing disruption to stock supply."

Error Type — Too Vague (TV) Answers

Analyse/Explain questions on ethics, environment, customer service, quality, location
Why students get it wrong: Students write generic statements without developing the business impact. Mark schemes flag many specific TV (Too Vague) answers: "May need to be more environmentally friendly" (TV for climate change question); "To help protect the environment" (TV for environmental reasons); "To keep customers satisfied" (TV for customer service); "Competition", "population", "customers" (TV for location factors); "Customer service getting better or worse" (TV for customer service analyse questions).
Correct approach: Always chain: Point → Business Impact → Financial/Strategic Consequence. Example instead of "being ethical improves reputation" write: "Acting ethically, such as paying fair wages, can improve DM's reputation (K), which may attract customers who value sustainability (APP), increasing sales revenue and market share (ANALYSIS)."

Error Type — Net Profit Margin Calculation Errors

Calculate [business]'s net profit margin for [year] to two decimal places
Why students get it wrong: Students use the inverse formula (Revenue ÷ Net Profit instead of Net Profit ÷ Revenue × 100), forget to multiply by 100, or fail to give the answer to exactly two decimal places. The 2025 mark scheme specifically flags "No reward for inverse answer (627.54%)" and awards maximum 1 mark if not given to two decimal places.
Correct formula: Net Profit Margin = (Net Profit ÷ Revenue) × 100. Always show workings. Always give to two decimal places unless told otherwise. Check: the answer should be less than 100%. Gross profit margin uses gross profit (before expenses) not net profit.

Error Type — Average Rate of Return (ARR) Formula

Calculate the average rate of return (ARR) for [investment]
Why students get it wrong: Students confuse total profit with average annual profit. They forget to divide total net profit by the number of years first, then divide by the initial investment. Some use revenue instead of profit. Others miss the "× 100" step to express as a percentage.
Correct formula: ARR = (Average Annual Profit ÷ Cost of Investment) × 100. Step 1: Average annual profit = Total net profit ÷ Number of years. Step 2: ARR = (Step 1 result ÷ Initial cost) × 100. Always express as a percentage (%).

Error Type — Break-even Calculation Units

Calculate the break-even quantity / point for [business]
Why students get it wrong: Students give the answer in £ (pounds) rather than units/sessions/items. The mark scheme notes "NB: Maximum of two marks for an answer with the wrong units (e.g. £)." Students also use total variable costs rather than variable cost per unit in the contribution formula.
Correct formula: BEQ = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price per unit − Variable Cost per unit). The denominator is "contribution per unit." Answer must be expressed in units (number of items, sessions, hours, etc.) not in £. Always show full workings for 3–4 mark questions.

Error Type — Recommend Questions Without Comparison

Recommend whether [business] should use Option A or Option B
Why students get it wrong: Students write only about their chosen option without referencing the rejected alternative. The mark schemes consistently state: "NB: If two separate points are made, there must be a comparative link" and "justification is why one method is BETTER THAN the other." A recommendation that only says "Option A is good because…" cannot access the top mark without comparing it to Option B.
Correct structure: (1) State your recommendation clearly. (2) Give one reason why the chosen option is better. (3) Reference why the rejected option is worse/less suitable in this context. Use the case study data to justify. E.g., "Riley should relocate to a restaurant (1) as it would provide a stable, year-round trading location (reason), unlike the caravan which is limited by weather and footfall (comparative reference to rejected option)."

Error Type — Analyse Answers Without Business Impact

Analyse one advantage/disadvantage of [action] for [business]
Why students get it wrong: Students identify a relevant point but do not develop it into a business consequence. Mark schemes repeatedly state "NB: Analysis must be business-facing" and "No reward for only stating 'low costs' or 'costs will increase' without development." Writing "it will reduce costs" alone earns no more than a knowledge mark. Students must show the consequence of that change.
Correct 3-mark chain: Knowledge (what) → Application (to the business context) → Analysis (consequence for the business). E.g., "Using batch production allows Pukka Pies to produce large quantities at once (K), meaning they can spread the fixed costs of the factory across more pies (APP), which reduces the cost per unit and improves the gross profit margin (ANALYSIS)."

Error Type — Environmental vs Ethical Confusion

State/identify environmental OR ethical considerations
Why students get it wrong: Students mix up ethical and environmental considerations, giving ethical answers when asked for environmental ones and vice versa. The 2025 mark scheme states "No reward for answers about sustainability, ethics or legislation" when asked for environmental reasons to be environmentally friendly. Conversely, answering "reduce carbon emissions" when asked for an ethical consideration is also penalised.
Correct distinction: Environmental = impact on the natural world (carbon emissions, recycling, waste, pollution, renewable energy, packaging). Ethical = doing what is morally right with people (fair wages, no child labour, fair trade, supplier treatment, honesty with customers). Climate change is an environmental/economic issue — not an ethical consideration unless framed around worker treatment.

Error Type — Finance Function vs Other Functions

Identify tasks of the finance function / Which is NOT a finance function task?
Why students get it wrong: Students incorrectly assign tasks to finance that belong to other functions. E.g., "advertising price reductions" (marketing, not finance), "creating educational resources" (operations/HR, not finance), "producing GCSE results reports" (data/admin, not finance). The finance function specifically covers: managing cash flow, budgeting, producing profit/loss accounts, calculating break-even, authorising payments, financial forecasting.
Finance function tasks include: Applying for loans, calculating break-even, measuring profit/loss, authorising supplier payments, producing financial reports, monitoring cash flow forecasts, managing budgets, calculating ARR for investment decisions. Marketing, HR hiring, and production scheduling are NOT finance function tasks.

Error Type — Evaluate Questions Without Balanced Judgement

Evaluate [topic] — 7-mark or 9-mark starred questions
Why students get it wrong: Students write one-sided responses arguing only advantages or only disadvantages. For 7-mark and 9-mark evaluate questions, the mark scheme requires a balanced discussion with a supported judgement. Students often reach L2 (4–5 marks) but cannot access L3 (6–7 marks) or L4 (8–9 marks) because they do not provide a clear, justified conclusion that weighs competing factors.
Correct structure for 9★ Evaluate: (1) Argument for (with K, APP, ANALYSIS) → (2) Counter-argument or limiting factor (with K, APP, ANALYSIS) → (3) Further development → (4) Balanced judgement referencing both sides and the specific context/data given. The conclusion must not introduce new points — it should synthesise what has been argued. Use phrases like "On balance…", "Taking into account…", "The most significant factor is… because in the context of [business]…"

Error Type — Not Using Case Study Context in Application

Any Analyse or higher question in Section B
Why students get it wrong: Students give textbook answers without relating them to the specific business in the question. Mark schemes state "NB: Context must be used to be awarded as application" and warn that general answers can access Knowledge but not Application marks. For example, writing "businesses use procurement to choose suppliers" without referencing that Gymshark/DM/Pukka Pies is a multinational/specific type of business loses the application mark.
Always name the business and its specific situation: Use the data provided (revenue figures, profit margins, production method, company type). For example: "For Gymshark, which is a fast-growing online sportswear brand (APP), using retained profits avoids the need to pay interest on a bank loan (K), which is significant given the economic pressures on consumer spending noted in the case study (ANALYSIS)."

6.3 Globalisation — Q18 Starred Evaluate (9 marks)
TOPIC 6.3.2 | Command: Evaluate | 9 marks | Highest Confidence
Evaluate how globalisation may affect [business name]'s decisions about where to locate its operations. [9]
★ Evaluate | 9 marks | Q18
Globalisation was the Q18 topic in 2020 (risk/reward for AM plc shareholders). No Q18 topic has been repeated, and with 5 years since 2020, globalisation is statistically overdue for return to Q18. The 2025 Q18 used economic climate; the 2021 Q18 used environmental considerations; globalisation fills the natural gap. Its link to location decisions (4.5) and MNC advantages (6.3.2) gives rich context for a business case study question.
Mark-Band Model Answer

L1 Knowledge points to include: Globalisation refers to the increasing integration of economies worldwide through trade, investment and technology. Businesses can locate operations in countries with lower costs, access new markets, or be closer to raw material sources.

L2 Application — Link to the Case Study Business: Reference specific details: type of business (MNC/plc), revenue data, existing international operations, supply chain details as given in the case study text.

L3 Developed Analysis — Arguments FOR globalisation influencing location:

  • Lower labour costs in developing countries allow [business] to reduce production costs, improving the gross profit margin and allowing more competitive pricing in a difficult economic climate.
  • Locating in a market close to where products are sold reduces logistics costs and delivery times, improving customer service and reducing distribution expenses.
  • Access to specialist skills or raw materials in specific countries (e.g., tech expertise in India, lithium in Chile) can improve product quality or reduce input costs.

L3 Counter-analysis — Challenges of global location:

  • Cultural and language barriers can increase management complexity and communication costs, reducing operational efficiency.
  • Political instability or changing trade regulations in overseas locations create supply chain risks that can disrupt production — a potential significant cost.
  • Ethical concerns about labour standards in some countries may damage the business's reputation with UK/EU consumers, reducing sales.

L4 Balanced Judgement: On balance, globalisation significantly influences location decisions primarily because cost reduction and market access advantages typically outweigh the risks for established multinationals with strong supply chain management. However, for smaller businesses or those with strong brand identities built on ethical sourcing, the reputational risks of offshore production may outweigh the cost savings. The most important factor depends on whether [business]'s competitive advantage is built on price or on brand/quality — if the former, global location is highly beneficial; if the latter, domestic production may better serve long-term objectives.

5.3 + 5.4 Finance: ARR Calculation + Break-even Analysis
TOPIC 5.3.6 + 5.4.2 | Command: Calculate + Analyse | 3+3 marks | Q17
(c)(i) Calculate the average rate of return (ARR) for [investment]. Show your workings. [3]
(ii) Calculate the break-even quantity for [business]. Show your workings. [3]
(iii) Analyse one way [business] could use its break-even data to make decisions. [3]
Calculate + Analyse | 3+3+3 marks | Q17
ARR and break-even have both appeared together in Q17 multiple times (2019, 2022, 2024). The combination of ARR calculation followed by break-even calculation and analysis is the most tested multi-calculation format in Paper 2. With the 2025 Q17 including ARR briefly, a fuller multi-calculation Q17 is anticipated in 2026, matching the 2019/2022 pattern.
Model Answers

ARR Calculation (3 marks):

Step 1: Average annual profit = Total net profit over life of investment ÷ Number of years
Step 2: ARR = (Average annual profit ÷ Initial cost of investment) × 100
Example: Investment costs £40,000; total net profit over 4 years = £28,000
Average annual profit = £28,000 ÷ 4 = £7,000
ARR = (£7,000 ÷ £40,000) × 100 = 17.5%

Break-even Calculation (3 marks):

BEQ = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price per unit − Variable Cost per unit)
BEQ = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution per unit
Example: FC = £12,000/month; SP = £40; VC = £16
Contribution = £40 − £16 = £24
BEQ = £12,000 ÷ £24 = 500 units

Analyse use of BEP (3 marks — full chain):

The business can use its break-even quantity to set sales targets (K). For [business], which is a small sole trader, knowing it must sell 500 units per month means Riley can set a minimum weekly sales target of approximately 125 units (APP). If sales fall below this, the business knows it is making a loss and can take action such as reducing costs or increasing prices before the problem becomes critical (ANALYSIS — business-facing consequence).

The sources rotate: crowdfunding, bank loan, share issue, selling assets, new partner have all been used. The most overdue combination for 2026 is crowdfunding vs retained profit or share issue vs bank loan, as these combinations haven't been tested since 2020/2024 respectively.
Model Answer — Crowdfunding vs Bank Loan

Advantage of Crowdfunding (3 marks):
Crowdfunding does not require repayment of interest (K). For a small business like [business] which is already managing tight cash flow (APP), avoiding interest payments means more cash is retained to cover operating costs such as wages and rent (ANALYSIS — business-facing).

Disadvantage of Crowdfunding (3 marks):
The business must make its business idea public to attract investors (K). This means that competitors could see [business]'s plans for expansion (APP), potentially copying the idea or undercutting prices before the expansion is complete, which could reduce the competitive advantage gained (ANALYSIS).

Recommend (3 marks — must compare both options):
[Business] should use a bank loan rather than crowdfunding (recommendation). Although a bank loan requires interest repayments, the business has a stable and proven cash flow (reference to data) meaning it can afford the monthly repayments without cash flow problems (reason chosen is better). Crowdfunding is unpredictable — there is no guarantee the target amount will be raised — so a bank loan offers more certainty of obtaining the full £30,000 needed (comparative reference to why rejected option is worse).

Therefore, ethics should be seen as a long-term enabler of profit rather than a barrier to it.
